Back when I was a translation student, I somehow got the job of dubbing a documentary.
I had no idea what I was getting into, but I agreed to work with two other translators and have the job done in a few weeks.
We encountered so many challenges that we had to give up the project and apologize to the client for wasting her time.
This was very discouraging, but I realized none of the problems had to do with our ability to translate. So what was stopping us?

We realized a few things:

  • We had no idea how to record a voice, let alone matching it to a video.
  • There were three of us and more than 20 different characters.
  • Translating from audio to text takes so long that it's even faster to make a transcript and then translate.
  • We had no way to keep the music and sound design while quieting the original voice tracks.

Years later, I worked for a video production company, where I became familiar with a lot of processes, software and techniques that would have been handy.
Even mastering these, that particular project was too ambitious, but I would have had the sense to turn it down.

What I attempted to do with this workshop was to give this same experience to other translation students and make them feel empowered, instead of discouraged. I wanted them to be able to choose projects wisely and to deliver a good product.

I started by researching best practices and trying different tools and steps until I developed a workflow that was both efficient and easy to learn. I structured all the valuable insights I had gained and I designed the workshop so that the students could learn from one another with a hands-on approach.

So I had a valuable system. I just needed students.

I used my web development skills to create a one-page that would describe the course and sign people up and I uploaded it to my website.
